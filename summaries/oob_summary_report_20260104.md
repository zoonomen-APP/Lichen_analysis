Summary of transposition errors.

That’s a good place to land — and yes, there’s actually a well‑studied set of **common numerical transcription errors** when humans copy data by hand or by eye.

Below is a **practical, experience‑based list**, grounded in data entry, cartography, surveying, and specimen label transcription.

---

## 1. Most common **single‑digit substitutions**

These happen because of **visual similarity**, **handwriting**, or **muscle memory**.

### Very common swaps
- **3 ↔ 5** ✅ (you’re right to suspect this)
- **1 ↔ 7**
- **0 ↔ 6**
- **2 ↔ 7**
- **8 ↔ 0**

### Less common but real
- **4 ↔ 9**
- **5 ↔ 6**
- **3 ↔ 8**

---

## 2. Sign errors (extremely common)

Not digit‑to‑digit, but **high‑impact**:

- **+ ↔ –** (latitude/longitude, elevations, balances)
- Missing minus sign entirely
- Trailing minus sign copied as leading minus

These are among the **most damaging** errors in spatial data.

---

## 3. Decimal point errors (very frequent)

Especially in coordinates and measurements:

- **Decimal dropped**  
  `44.375 → 44375`
- **Decimal shifted one place**  
  `44.375 → 4.4375`
- **Decimal duplicated**  
  `44..375`

In geographic coordinates, this can mean:
- Tens to hundreds of kilometers of error

---

## 4. Transposition errors (order swapped)

Classic human error:

- **12 → 21**
- **39 → 93**
- **406 → 460**

These are **more frequent than random chance**, especially for adjacent digits.

---

## 5. Repetition / omission errors

- **Digit repeated**  
  `47 → 477`
- **Digit omitted**  
  `104 → 10`

Often caused by eye jumping lines or fatigue.

---

## 6. Unit‑driven errors

Numbers copied correctly but **meaning changes**:

- Degrees ↔ minutes
- Feet ↔ meters
- Miles ↔ kilometers
- Seconds treated as decimals

Example:
- `44° 30′` copied as `44.30`

---

## 7. Context‑specific (coordinates & natural history data)

These come up *a lot* in herbarium and biodiversity datasets:

- **Latitude copied into longitude field**
- **Longitude truncated**
- **Minutes copied as decimal degrees**
- **Hemisphere letter dropped (N/S/E/W)**

---

## 8. Ranked by frequency (rough, practical ordering)

1. Decimal point errors  
2. Sign errors (+/–)  
3. Transposition of adjacent digits  
4. 1↔7, 3↔5 digit swaps  
5. Dropped or duplicated digits  
6. Unit confusion  

---

## 9. Why 3 ↔ 5 specifically is common

- Similar stroke patterns in handwriting
- Similar visual “weight”
- Often written with curves
- Common in both **coordinates** and **dates**

So your instinct there is **spot on**.

---

## 10. Practical takeaway

> Most numeric errors are **not random** — they cluster around:
> - signs
> - decimals
> - adjacent digits
> - visually similar numerals

This is why automated QA checks that *assume randomness* often miss the worst problems.
